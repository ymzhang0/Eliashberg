# Hubbard-Stratonovich Transformation Derivation



**1. Standard Gaussian Identity**

We start with the auxiliary Gaussian integral for a variable $y$:


\begin{equation}

    \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}y^2\right) dy = \sqrt{2\pi}

\end{equation}





**2. Shift Transformation**

Introduce a shift $y \to y - cx^2$ to couple $y$ with $x^2$. The integration measure remains unchanged ($dy$):


\begin{equation}

\begin{aligned}
    \sqrt{2\pi} &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}(y - cx^2)^2\right] dy \\
    &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}y^2 + cyx^2 - \frac{1}{2}c^2 x^4\right) dy \\
    &= \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}c^2 x^4\right) \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}y^2 + cyx^2\right) dy
\end{aligned}

\end{equation}



Rearranging to isolate the quartic term:


\begin{equation}

    \exp\left(\frac{1}{2}c^2 x^4\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}y^2 + cyx^2\right) dy

\end{equation}





**3. Coefficient Matching**

To match the target term $\exp(bx^4)$, we set $\frac{1}{2}c^2 = b$, which implies $c = \sqrt{2b}$. Substituting this back:


\begin{equation}

    \exp(bx^4) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}y^2 + \sqrt{2b}\,x^2 y\right) dy

\end{equation}





**4. Full Integral Transformation**

Substitute the above identity into the original integral $I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp(-ax^2 + bx^4) dx$:


\begin{equation}

\begin{aligned}
    I &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-ax^2} \left[ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\frac{1}{2}y^2 + \sqrt{2b}\,x^2 y} dy \right] dx \\
      &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dy \, e^{-\frac{1}{2}y^2} \underbrace{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dx \, \exp\left[ -\left(a - y\sqrt{2b}\right)x^2 \right]}_{\text{Gaussian integral over } x}
\end{aligned}

\end{equation}





**5. Integrating out $x$**

Performing the inner Gaussian integral over $x$:


\begin{equation}

    I = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}y^2\right) \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a - y\sqrt{2b}}} \, dy

\end{equation}


We can absorb the $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}$ into the Gaussian integral over $y$:

\begin{equation}

    I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\exp\left(-y^2\right)}{\sqrt{{a - 2\sqrt{b}y}}} \, dy

\end{equation}

**6. Saddle Point Approximation for the Integral with a Denominator**

We aim to approximate the following integral:

$$
I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dy \, \frac{e^{-y^2}}{\sqrt{a - 2\sqrt{b}y}}
$$

**7. Effective Action Formulation**

To perform a systematic expansion, we absorb the pre-factor (the denominator) into the exponent. We rewrite the integrand as $e^{-\Phi(y)}$, where $\Phi(y)$ is the total effective action:

$$
\Phi(y) = y^2 + \frac{1}{2} \ln(a - 2\sqrt{b}y)
$$

**8. Saddle Point Condition**

The saddle point $y_0$ is defined by the stationary condition of the action $\Phi'(y_0) = 0$.

**9. Derivation**
$$
\frac{d\Phi}{dy} = 2y + \frac{1}{2} \frac{-2\sqrt{b}}{a - 2\sqrt{b}y} = 2y - \frac{\sqrt{b}}{a - 2\sqrt{b}y} = 0
$$

Rearranging this yields a quadratic equation:
$$
2y(a - 2\sqrt{b}y) = \sqrt{b} \implies 4\sqrt{b}y^2 - 2ay + \sqrt{b} = 0
$$

**10. Solution**
Solving for $y$, and selecting the branch that remains finite (and close to 0) as $b \to 0$ (the perturbative limit):

$$
y_0 = \frac{a - \sqrt{a^2 - 4b}}{4\sqrt{b}}
$$

**11. Gaussian Fluctuations (Second Derivative)**

We expand $\Phi(y)$ to the second order around $y_0$:
$$
\Phi(y) \approx \Phi(y_0) + \frac{1}{2} \Phi''(y_0) (y - y_0)^2
$$

**12. Calculating $\Phi''$**
$$
\Phi''(y) = \frac{d}{dy} \left( 2y - \frac{\sqrt{b}}{a - 2\sqrt{b}y} \right) = 2 - \frac{2b}{(a - 2\sqrt{b}y)^2}
$$

**13. Simplification at $y_0$**
Using the saddle point condition $\frac{\sqrt{b}}{a - 2\sqrt{b}y_0} = 2y_0$, we substitute the fraction term:

$$
\Phi''(y_0) = 2 - 2 \left( \frac{\sqrt{b}}{a - 2\sqrt{b}y_0} \right)^2 = 2 - 2(2y_0)^2 = 2(1 - 4y_0^2)
$$

**14. Final Approximation Result**

Integrating the Gaussian fluctuations:
$$
I \approx e^{-\Phi(y_0)} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dy \, e^{-\frac{1}{2} \Phi''(y_0) (y - y_0)^2} = e^{-\Phi(y_0)} \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\Phi''(y_0)}}
$$

Substituting the explicit forms of $e^{-\Phi}$ and $\Phi''$:

1.  **Exponential Part:** $e^{-\Phi(y_0)} = \frac{e^{-y_0^2}}{\sqrt{a - 2\sqrt{b}y_0}}$
2.  **Fluctuation Part:** $\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{2(1 - 4y_0^2)}} = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{1 - 4y_0^2}}$

**Final Formula:**
$$
I \approx \frac{e^{-y_0^2}}{\sqrt{a - 2\sqrt{b}y_0}} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{1 - 4y_0^2}}
$$

*Validity Condition:* The approximation holds strictly when the effective curvature is positive, i.e., $1 - 4y_0^2 > 0$, or $y_0 < 0.5$.

# Interaction Expansion for a Two-State Fermion System

## 1. Model Setup

A more realistic example is that the field $\phi$ is a function of momentum $p$. 

Consider the interaction part of the action:

$$
S_{\text{int}} = \frac{C}{2} \sum_{p, p', q} \bar{\psi}_{p+q} \bar{\psi}_{p'-q} V(\mathbf{q}) \psi_{p'} \psi_p
$$

Where $C = T^3/L^3$ is the coefficient. For simplicity, we restrict the momentum space to two discrete states: $p, p' \in \{p_1, p_2\}$.

**Key Properties of Grassmann Numbers:**
* **Anti-commutativity:** $\psi_a \psi_b = - \psi_b \psi_a$
* **Nilpotency:** $\psi_a \psi_a = 0$ (Pauli exclusion principle)

---

## 2. Expansion of the Summation

Due to $\psi_p \psi_p = 0$, terms vanish when $p = p'$. We only need to sum over $p \neq p'$.
There are two symmetric cases: $(p=p_1, p'=p_2)$ and $(p=p_2, p'=p_1)$. We expand the first case in detail.

To ensure the output momentum indices remain within the set $\{p_1, p_2\}$, the momentum transfer $q$ can only take two specific values:

### Case A ($q=0$): Direct / Hartree Term
The output indices remain unchanged ($p_1 \to p_1, p_2 \to p_2$):
$$
\text{Term}_1 = \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \bar{\psi}_{p_2} V(0) \psi_{p_2} \psi_{p_1}
$$

### Case B ($q = p_2 - p_1$): Exchange / Fock Term
The output indices are swapped ($p_1 \to p_2, p_2 \to p_1$):
$$
\text{Term}_2 = \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \bar{\psi}_{p_1} V(p_2 - p_1) \psi_{p_2} \psi_{p_1}
$$

Using the anti-commutation relation $\bar{\psi}_{p_2} \bar{\psi}_{p_1} = - \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \bar{\psi}_{p_2}$, we rewrite the exchange term as:
$$
\text{Term}_2 = - V(p_2 - p_1) \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \psi_{p_2} \psi_{p_1}
$$

---

## 3. Resulting Effective Interaction

Summing all contributions (including the symmetric $p \leftrightarrow p'$ case, which cancels the factor $1/2$), the total interaction action becomes:

$$
S_{\text{int}}^{\text{2-state}} = C \underbrace{\left[ V(0) - V(p_2 - p_1) \right]}_{g_{\text{eff}}} \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \psi_{p_2} \psi_{p_1}
$$

---

## 4. Hubbard-Stratonovich Transformation

The four-fermion interaction term can be decoupled in the Density, Exchange and Cooper channels. These alternative schemes reveal different physical instabilities, such as Charge Density Waves (CDW) or Ferromagnetism or Superconductivity.

### Direct / Hartree Channel

We use **Fierz-like rearrangements** to rewrite Eq.12 before applying the Hubbard-Stratonovich (HS) transformation.

This channel focuses on the interaction between particle densities $\hat{n}_p = \bar{\psi}_p \psi_p$.

#### Operator Rearrangement
We commute $\psi_{p_2}$ and $\psi_{p_1}$ (generating a minus sign) to group operators by their momentum index:

$$
S_{\text{int}} = - C g_{\text{eff}} (\bar{\psi}_{p_1} \psi_{p_1}) (\bar{\psi}_{p_2} \psi_{p_2})
$$

Using the algebraic identity for densities $n_1 n_2 \approx \frac{1}{2}(n_1 + n_2)^2$ (ignoring $n^2$ terms which only shift the chemical potential), we approximate the interaction as a square of the total density:

$$
S_{\text{int}}^{\text{dens}} \approx - \frac{C g_{\text{eff}}}{2} \left( \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \psi_{p_1} + \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \psi_{p_2} \right)^2
$$

#### HS Transformation
We introduce a real auxiliary scalar field $\phi(\tau)$ to decouple the quadratic term.
* **Field Interpretation:** $\phi$ acts as a fluctuating electrostatic potential (Hartree field).

$$
\exp\left( -S_{\text{int}}^{\text{dens}} \right) = \int \mathcal{D}\phi \exp \left[ - \int d\tau \left( \frac{\phi^2}{2 C g_{\text{eff}}} + i\phi (\bar{\psi}_{p_1} \psi_{p_1} + \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \psi_{p_2}) \right) \right]
$$

#### Fermionic Path Integral
The total fermionic action becomes diagonal in momentum space (no mixing between $p_1$ and $p_2$):

$$
S_{\text{fermion}} = \sum_{\omega_n} \left[ \bar{\psi}_{p_1} (-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1} + i\phi) \psi_{p_1} + \bar{\psi}_{p_2} (-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_2} + i\phi) \psi_{p_2} \right]
$$

Since the matrix is diagonal, the determinant is simply the product of the integrals for each state.

#### Effective Action
Integrating out $\psi_{p_1}$ and $\psi_{p_2}$:

$$
S_{\text{eff}}[\phi] = \frac{\beta \phi^2}{2 C g_{\text{eff}}} - \sum_n \ln(-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1} + i\phi) - \sum_n \ln(-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_2} + i\phi)
$$

---

### Exchange Channel (Magnetic / Fock)

This channel focuses on the scattering of a particle from state $p_1$ to $p_2$ and vice versa, often related to magnetic instabilities.

#### Operator Rearrangement
We rewrite the interaction to pair $\bar{\psi}_{p_1}$ with $\psi_{p_2}$.
Starting from $\bar{\psi}_{p_1} \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \psi_{p_2} \psi_{p_1}$:
1.  Swap $\bar{\psi}_{p_2}$ and $\psi_{p_2} \to - \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \psi_{p_2} \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \psi_{p_1}$ (plus a constant).
2.  Group terms as transition operators:

$$
S_{\text{int}}^{\text{exch}} = - C g_{\text{eff}} (\bar{\psi}_{p_1} \psi_{p_2}) (\bar{\psi}_{p_2} \psi_{p_1})
$$

#### HS Transformation
We introduce a complex auxiliary bosonic field $M(\tau)$ (and its conjugate $\bar{M}$) to decouple the term.
* **Field Interpretation:** $M$ represents a "hybridization" or spin-flip field derived from the Fock exchange potential.

$$
\exp\left( -S_{\text{int}}^{\text{exch}} \right) = \int \mathcal{D}M \mathcal{D}\bar{M} \exp \left[ - \int d\tau \left( \frac{|M|^2}{C g_{\text{eff}}} - M \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \psi_{p_1} - \bar{M} \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \psi_{p_2} \right) \right]
$$

#### Fermionic Path Integral
The fermionic action now mixes $p_1$ and $p_2$. We write it in vector form $\Psi = (\psi_{p_1}, \psi_{p_2})^T$:

$$
S_{\text{fermion}} = \sum_{\omega_n} \begin{pmatrix} \bar{\psi}_{p_1} & \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \end{pmatrix}
\underbrace{
\begin{pmatrix}
-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1} & -\bar{M} \\
-M & -i\omega_n + \xi_{p_2}
\end{pmatrix}
}_{\mathcal{G}^{-1}_{\text{exch}}}
\begin{pmatrix}
\psi_{p_1} \\
\psi_{p_2}
\end{pmatrix}
$$

#### Effective Action
We calculate the determinant of the $2 \times 2$ matrix:
$$
\det(\mathcal{G}^{-1}_{\text{exch}}) = (-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1})(-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_2}) - |M|^2
$$

Integrating out the fermions yields:

$$
S_{\text{eff}}[M] = \frac{\beta |M|^2}{C g_{\text{eff}}} - \sum_n \ln \left[ (-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1})(-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_2}) - |M|^2 \right]
$$

---

### Cooper Channel

To decouple the quartic term, we define **Pairing Operators** (analogous to Cooper pairs):

$$
\begin{aligned}
\hat{\Delta} &= \psi_{p_2} \psi_{p_1} \\
\hat{\Delta}^\dagger &= \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \bar{\psi}_{p_2}
\end{aligned}
$$

The action is now written in a perfect square form suitable for the HS transformation:

$$
S_{\text{int}}^{\text{2-state}} = C g_{\text{eff}} \, \hat{\Delta}^\dagger \hat{\Delta}
$$

Using the complex Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation formula:

$$
e^{ - C g_{\text{eff}} \hat{\Delta}^\dagger \hat{\Delta} } \propto \int \mathcal{D}\Delta \mathcal{D}\bar{\Delta} \exp\left[ \frac{|\Delta|^2}{C g_{\text{eff}}} - \left( \bar{\Delta} \psi_{p_2} \psi_{p_1} + \Delta \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \right) \right]
$$

This successfully linearizes the fermion interaction by introducing an auxiliary bosonic field $\Delta$.

#### Total Action and Path Integral (Explicit Form)

Here, we construct the full path integral by explicitly writing out the free fermion terms and the Hubbard-Stratonovich interaction terms without using matrix notation.


The total action $S_{\text{total}}$ consists of three distinct parts:
$$
S_{\text{total}} = S_{\text{free}} + S_{\text{boson}} + S_{\text{coupling}}
$$

This describes the kinetic energy and chemical potential for the fermions in states $p_1$ and $p_2$. In imaginary time $\tau$:

$$
S_{\text{free}} = \int_0^\beta d\tau \left[ \bar{\psi}_{p_1} (\partial_\tau + \xi_{p_1}) \psi_{p_1} + \bar{\psi}_{p_2} (\partial_\tau + \xi_{p_2}) \psi_{p_2} \right]
$$

* $\xi_p = \epsilon_p - \mu$
* We sum over the two specific momentum states $p_1$ and $p_2$.

This term comes purely from the HS transformation and acts as the "mass term" or cost function for the auxiliary field $\Delta$:

$$
S_{\text{boson}} = \int_0^\beta d\tau \frac{|\Delta(\tau)|^2}{C g_{\text{eff}}}
$$

This term describes the physical process where fermions interact with the pairing field.
* $\bar{\Delta} \psi_{p_2} \psi_{p_1}$: The field $\bar{\Delta}$ destroys a pair of fermions (annihilates $p_1, p_2$).
* $\Delta \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \bar{\psi}_{p_2}$: The field $\Delta$ creates a pair of fermions (creates $p_1, p_2$).

$$
S_{\text{coupling}} = - \int_0^\beta d\tau \left( \bar{\Delta}(\tau) \psi_{p_2}(\tau) \psi_{p_1}(\tau) + \Delta(\tau) \bar{\psi}_{p_1}(\tau) \bar{\psi}_{p_2}(\tau) \right)
$$

---

#### The Final Explicit Path Integral

Combining all terms above, the partition function $Z$ is the integral over all fermion fields ($\psi, \bar{\psi}$) and the auxiliary boson field ($\Delta, \bar{\Delta}$):

$$
Z = \int \mathcal{D}\Delta \mathcal{D}\bar{\Delta} \mathcal{D}\bar{\psi}_{p_1} \mathcal{D}\psi_{p_1} \mathcal{D}\bar{\psi}_{p_2} \mathcal{D}\psi_{p_2} \exp\left( -S_{\text{total}} \right)
$$

With the full explicit action:

$$
\begin{aligned}
S_{\text{total}} = \int_0^\beta d\tau \bigg[ \quad
& \underbrace{\bar{\psi}_{p_1} (\partial_\tau + \xi_{p_1}) \psi_{p_1} + \bar{\psi}_{p_2} (\partial_\tau + \xi_{p_2}) \psi_{p_2}}_{\text{Normal Propagation}} \\
&+ \underbrace{\frac{\bar{\Delta} \Delta}{C g_{\text{eff}}}}_{\text{Auxiliary Field Cost}} \\
&- \underbrace{\left( \bar{\Delta} \psi_{p_2} \psi_{p_1} + \Delta \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \right)}_{\text{Pair Creation / Annihilation}}
\quad \bigg]
\end{aligned}
$$

#### Frequency Domain Representation

If we expand in Matsubara frequencies ($\partial_\tau \to -i\omega_n$), the action becomes a sum over $n$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
S_{\text{total}} = \frac{\beta |\Delta|^2}{C g_{\text{eff}}} + \sum_{n} \bigg[ \quad
& \bar{\psi}_{p_1, n} (-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1}) \psi_{p_1, n} \\
+ \, & \bar{\psi}_{p_2, n} (-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_2}) \psi_{p_2, n} \\
- \, & \bar{\Delta} \psi_{p_2, -n} \psi_{p_1, n} - \Delta \bar{\psi}_{p_1, n} \bar{\psi}_{p_2, -n}
\quad \bigg]
\end{aligned}
$$
*(Assuming $\Delta$ is static/constant for simplicity here, otherwise $\Delta$ would carry frequency transfer).*


#### Sequential Integration of Fermion Fields

Instead of using matrix determinants, we integrate the fields one by one. We start by integrating out the field $\psi_{p_1}$ (and $\bar{\psi}_{p_1}$), treating $\psi_{p_2}$ as a background source.

#### Isolating the $p_1$ Terms

Let's look at the action for a single frequency $n$. We group all terms containing $\psi_{p_1}$ or $\bar{\psi}_{p_1}$:

$$
S_{\text{total}} = S_{p_1} + S_{p_2}
$$

Where $S_{p_1}$ contains all the dependence on particle 1:

$$
S_{p_1} = \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \underbrace{(-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1})}_{A_1} \psi_{p_1} - \Delta \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \bar{\psi}_{p_2} - \bar{\Delta} \psi_{p_2} \psi_{p_1}
$$

And $S_{p_2}$ is the remaining term for particle 2:

$$
S_{p_2} = \bar{\psi}_{p_2} (-i\omega_{-n} + \xi_{p_2}) \psi_{p_2}
$$

#### Completing the Square for $\psi_{p_1}$

To integrate $\int \mathcal{D}\bar{\psi}_{p_1} \mathcal{D}\psi_{p_1} e^{-S_{p_1}}$, we need to rewrite the interaction terms as "sources" ($\eta$) coupled to the fields.

#### Rearranging Interaction Terms
We need the form $\bar{\psi}_{p_1} \eta + \bar{\eta} \psi_{p_1}$.

1.  **Term 1:** $- \Delta \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \bar{\psi}_{p_2} = \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \underbrace{(-\Delta \bar{\psi}_{p_2})}_{\eta}$
2.  **Term 2:** $- \bar{\Delta} \psi_{p_2} \psi_{p_1} = + \bar{\Delta} \psi_{p_1} \psi_{p_2} = \underbrace{(\bar{\Delta} \psi_{p_2})}_{\bar{\eta}} \psi_{p_1}$
    *(Note: We swapped $\psi_{p_2}$ and $\psi_{p_1}$ causing a sign change, then grouped to the left).*

So the action for $p_1$ is:
$$
S_{p_1} = \bar{\psi}_{p_1} A_1 \psi_{p_1} + \bar{\psi}_{p_1} \eta + \bar{\eta} \psi_{p_1}
$$
With sources:
* $\eta = -\Delta \bar{\psi}_{p_2}$
* $\bar{\eta} = \bar{\Delta} \psi_{p_2}$

#### Performing the Integral
Using the Gaussian shift formula $\int e^{-(\bar{\psi} A \psi + \bar{\psi} \eta + \bar{\eta} \psi)} = \det(A) e^{\bar{\eta} A^{-1} \eta}$:

$$
\int \mathcal{D}\bar{\psi}_{p_1} \mathcal{D}\psi_{p_1} e^{-S_{p_1}} = \det(A_1) \exp\left( \bar{\eta} A_1^{-1} \eta \right)
$$

#### The Induced Term for $p_2$

Now we calculate the shift term $\bar{\eta} A_1^{-1} \eta$ which will be added to the action of $p_2$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Shift} &= (\bar{\Delta} \psi_{p_2}) \frac{1}{-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1}} (-\Delta \bar{\psi}_{p_2}) \\
&= - \frac{|\Delta|^2}{-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1}} \psi_{p_2} \bar{\psi}_{p_2}
\end{aligned}
$$

We swap $\psi_{p_2}$ and $\bar{\psi}_{p_2}$ to match standard order (adding a minus sign):

$$
\text{Shift} = + \frac{|\Delta|^2}{-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1}} \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \psi_{p_2}
$$

#### Integrating $\psi_{p_2}$

Now we construct the effective action for the remaining fermion $p_2$.
The total coefficient for $\bar{\psi}_{p_2} \psi_{p_2}$ is the original kinetic term plus the new shift term derived above.

$$
S_{\text{eff}}^{(p_2)} = \bar{\psi}_{p_2} \underbrace{\left[ (i\omega_n + \xi_{p_2}) + \frac{|\Delta|^2}{-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1}} \right]}_{A_{\text{new}}} \psi_{p_2}
$$
*(Note: $-i\omega_{-n}$ became $i\omega_n$)*.

Now, integrate out $\psi_{p_2}$:
$$
\int \mathcal{D}\bar{\psi}_{p_2} \mathcal{D}\psi_{p_2} e^{-S_{\text{eff}}^{(p_2)}} = \det(A_{\text{new}})
$$

#### Final Result Check

The total partition function is the product of the two determinants we found:

$$
\begin{aligned}
Z &\propto \det(A_1) \times \det(A_{\text{new}}) \\
&= (-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1}) \times \left[ (i\omega_n + \xi_{p_2}) + \frac{|\Delta|^2}{-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1}} \right] \\
&= (-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1})(i\omega_n + \xi_{p_2}) + |\Delta|^2
\end{aligned}
$$

This perfectly matches the determinant of the matrix derived in the previous method:
$$
\det \mathcal{M} = (i\omega_n - \xi_{p_1})(i\omega_n + \xi_{p_2}) - |\Delta|^2
$$
*(Note: Since $-i\omega_n + \xi_{p_1} = -(i\omega_n - \xi_{p_1})$, the product is $-(i\omega - \xi)(i\omega + \xi) + |\Delta|^2 = - [ (i\omega - \xi)(i\omega + \xi) - |\Delta|^2 ]$. The overall sign is usually absorbed or consistent).*

#### Final Effective Action for the Pairing Field $\Delta$

After integrating out all fermionic degrees of freedom ($\psi_{p_1}, \psi_{p_2}$), we arrive at a theory involving only the bosonic order parameter $\Delta$.

#### The Partition Function

The partition function is now a path integral over the complex scalar field $\Delta$:

$$
Z = \int \mathcal{D}\Delta \mathcal{D}\bar{\Delta} \exp\left( - S_{\text{eff}}[\Delta] \right)
$$

#### The Effective Action ($S_{\text{eff}}$)

Combining the original quadratic cost term and the contribution from the fermion determinant derived in the previous steps:

$$
S_{\text{eff}}[\Delta] = S_{\text{condense}} + S_{\text{fluct}}
$$

#### Condensation Term (Mean-Field Cost)
This comes from the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation itself:
$$
S_{\text{condense}} = \frac{\beta |\Delta|^2}{C g_{\text{eff}}}
$$

#### Fluctuation Term (Fermionic Determinant)
This term represents the free energy of the fermions moving in the background of the $\Delta$ field. Using the identity $\det(A) = \exp(\text{Tr} \ln A)$, or simply summing the log of the determinant eigenvalues we found:

$$
S_{\text{fluct}} = - \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \ln \left[ (\omega_n^2 + \xi_{p_1} \xi_{p_2} + |\Delta|^2) + i\omega_n(\xi_{p_2} - \xi_{p_1}) \right]
$$
*(Note: We used the result $\det = (i\omega - \xi_1)(i\omega + \xi_2) - |\Delta|^2$ and simplified signs inside the log).*

#### The Symmetric Case (Standard BCS Result)

If we assume the two states are degenerate or related by time-reversal symmetry such that $\xi_{p_1} = \xi_{p_2} = \xi$:

$$
S_{\text{eff}}[\Delta] = \frac{\beta |\Delta|^2}{C g_{\text{eff}}} - \sum_{n} \ln \left[ \beta^2 (\omega_n^2 + \underbrace{\xi^2 + |\Delta|^2}_{E^2}) \right]
$$

* $E = \sqrt{\xi^2 + |\Delta|^2}$ is the **Quasiparticle Excitation Energy**.
* The term $\beta^2$ is usually a constant factor absorbed into the measure.

#### Evaluation of the Frequency Sum (Optional)

We can explicitly perform the Matsubara summation $T \sum_n \ln(\omega_n^2 + E^2)$ to get the thermodynamic potential $\Omega$:

$$
S_{\text{eff}}[\Delta] \approx \beta \Omega = \beta \left[ \frac{|\Delta|^2}{C g_{\text{eff}}} - 2 T \ln \left( 2 \cosh \frac{\beta E}{2} \right) \right]
$$

Or in the zero-temperature limit ($\beta \to \infty$):
$$
E_{\text{ground}} = \frac{|\Delta|^2}{C g_{\text{eff}}} - E
$$
*(Minimizing this gives the Gap Equation).*
